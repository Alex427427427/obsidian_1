They sat on the kitchen stools. The table leg wobbled. It still wobbled. A tram rattled past outside, sparks leaping from the wire - small proofs of friction. 

Engineer: If we get married, I will finally fix this table properly. Right now it's usable but intolerable at the same time. 

Physicist: You already fixed it twice. Each time you claimed to be the final time. 

Engineer: Too many degrees of freedom. We need boundary conditions. Marriage provides the appropriate constraints. 

Physicist: What a terrible proposal! "Will you marry me so I can impose constraints on your furniture?"

Engineer: Also on my life. Otherwise it oscillates. Unstable system. I've done the math, you see. 

The physicist smiled, pushing a strand of hair back with her hand. Chalk dust still clung to her sleeve from the Fraunhofer Institute. The smile turned into concern. 

Physicist: The math always assumes ideal conditions. No air resistance, no shortages, no Central Committee meetings. What stability can be found in this world?

Engineer: Committees are just noise. You average them out. 

Physicist: You can't average out power.

Engineer: We can keep our distance from it. I married my equations years ago. They're faithful. The outside world on the other hand... it keeps cheating. 

Physicist: Reality has a high interaction cross section. You knew that. 

They listened to the kettle click off. The engineer poured his tea too strong. The physicist diluted hers without comment. 

Physicist: My advisor says love is like a machine: if you neglect it, entropy runs it down. 

Engineer: My colleague says love is like entropy; it always increases. 

Physicist: Your colleague is an optimist.

Engineer: He survived three purges and still believes in industrial standards. He is a realist. 

Physicist: ...

Engineer: Entropy increasing doesn't mean heat death tomorrow. It just means you have to do work. 

Physicist: Exactly. Work costs energy. Energy is scarce. 

Engineer: Not if you build better machines and engines. 

Physicist: You can't beat Carnot.

Engineer: I can try. That's the engineer's oath. 

The physicist laughed. Then, she grew quiet, staring into the tea. 

Physicist: I'm afraid of small lives. Of waking up at fifty and discovering we optimised for safety. No risk, no insight. 

Engineer: I'm afraid of large failures. Of systems that collapse because someone chased elegance instead of robustness. 

Physicist: You're saying I'm reckless.

Engineer: I'm saying you're beautiful when you're reckless. Terrifying, but beautiful. 

Physicist: And you're beautiful when you're cautious. Like a bridge that doesn't sing in the wind. 

Engineer: Bridges that sing still stand.

Physicist, longingly and nervously staring into the engineer's eyes: Until resonance finds them. 

The engineer reached across the table. He steadied the wobble with his palm. 

Engineer: Listen. We live in a Union that loves plans and five-year horizons. It promises certainty and delivers improvisation. Marriage won't make us free of that. But it gives us a local lab. A place to test hypotheses without submitting a proposal. 

Physicist: Peer review will be brutal. 

Engineer: Only one reviewer. You. Relentless and with integrity. 

Physicist: I reject papers with sloppy assumptions. 

Engineer: Then help me state them clearly. Assumption one: we are finite. Assumption two: meaning is not handed down like spare parts. We assemble it, with our own hands and imperfect tools.

Physicist: Assumption three: joy can be created and destroyed. 

Engineer: I like that. We'll need a factory. 

The physicist reached out for the engineer's hand. 

Physicist: Do you ever think about how small we are? Protons arguing in a lattice of history? 

Engineer: Like Einstein showed us, I can be your antimatter. I'll find you in the lattice, and together we will become light, escaping into infinity.  

Physicist: That was Dirac. 

Engineer: My bad. But... yea, I do. Constantly. That's why I want something solid. A bolt tightened to spec. Even if the whole structure fails someday, that bolt did its job. 

Physicist: I want something true, even if its provisional. A theory that works until it doesn't.

Engineer: We'll log the failures. 

Physicist: And we'll laugh at them later, when the data is kind. 

The tram passed again, close now. The lights flickered. 

Engineer: So. Marriage.

Physicist: Mm.

They clicked their cups. 

Physicist: Hypothesis?

Engineer: That two people, under pressure, can lower each other's noise floor.

Physicist: Expected outcome? 

Engineer: Fewer errors. More signal. 

She nodded. 

Physicist: I can work with that.

She squeezed his hand.
